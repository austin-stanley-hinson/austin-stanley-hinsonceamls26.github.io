what_i_learned: |
  Today, I continued strengthening the BBB portion of my Alzheimer's drug
  discovery workflow. I reviewed the classification, regression, and
  promising-candidate modeling tasks to better understand how each part fits into
  the overall QSAR pipeline.

  For BBB classification, I confirmed that the model predicts whether compounds
  are BBB-positive or BBB-negative. For regression, I worked with logBB values to
  estimate quantitative brain-to-blood distribution. I also refined the
  promising-candidate workflow by correcting a rule-leakage issue. The teaching
  label was created using RDKit descriptor criteria, but the final promising
  model was changed to use ECFP4 fingerprints only so that the model learns
  structural patterns instead of directly memorizing the descriptor thresholds.

  I also worked on preparing an external validation dataset for Ramisa using the
  MoleculeNet BBBP dataset. I standardized the structures with RDKit, generated
  canonical SMILES and InChIKeys, and removed compounds that overlapped with our
  existing B3DB BBB dataset. After removing overlaps, I found enough
  non-overlapping compounds to create a balanced external validation set with 100
  BBB-positive and 100 BBB-negative compounds.

  Later, I presented at the weekly showcase and shared progress from my BBB
  modeling workflow, including classification, regression, promising-label
  prioritization, and external validation preparation.

blockers: |
  The main challenge today was making sure the promising-candidate workflow did
  not accidentally leak the teaching-rule criteria into the final model. I had to
  carefully separate the descriptor-based label creation from the fingerprint-based
  model training so the model could learn structural patterns more fairly.

reflection: |
  Today helped me better understand the difference between predicting BBB
  permeability, estimating logBB values, and prioritizing promising compounds.
  Preparing the external validation set also made the workflow feel stronger
  because it gives us a way to test whether the BBB models generalize to unseen
  compounds outside our original B3DB dataset.
