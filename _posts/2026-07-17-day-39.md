what_i_learned: |
  Today I focused on turning our Alzheimer's drug discovery workflow into a
  stronger research-paper methodology section. I reviewed the endpoint-specific
  drafts from the team and organized the methods around the major parts of the
  pipeline: dataset curation, molecular representation, target-specific QSAR
  modeling, internal validation, BBB and molecular-property filtering, and
  docking-based structural validation.

  A major part of the work was making sure the methodology stayed accurate and
  not overstated. I separated what was directly supported by our drafts from what
  still needs confirmation. For example, I clarified that AChE was modeled as a
  classification-only task, BACE1 used both classification and pIC50 regression,
  Tau/MAPT used active-vs-inconclusive classification plus pActivity regression,
  and BBB served as a permeability and drug-likeness filtering layer rather than
  a direct Alzheimer's activity model.

  I also helped draft several methodology subsections in LaTeX-style writing,
  including Overall Study Design, Molecular Representation, Target-Specific QSAR
  Modeling, Internal Model Validation, and Molecular Property and BBB Assessment.
  These sections explain how SMILES structures were standardized, how
  ECFP4/Morgan fingerprints and RDKit descriptors were used, how models were
  trained separately by endpoint, and how validation metrics were chosen carefully
  for imbalanced datasets.

  Later, I worked on the molecular docking methodology for the AChE
  structure-based validation stage. I confirmed that docking used the human AChE
  structure PDB 4EY7, which is co-crystallized with donepezil, and that donepezil
  was used to define the binding pocket. I summarized the four-tool docking
  workflow using AutoDock Vina 1.2.7, GNINA v1.3.3, rDock 2013.1, and DOCK6
  v6.13, along with the shared binding-site center and consensus-ranking
  strategy.

blockers: |
  The biggest challenge today was balancing technical detail with professional
  research writing. The computational workflow included many files, parameters,
  setup steps, and warning flags, but the paper needed to present those details
  clearly without sounding like a notebook dump.

reflection: |
  Today helped me understand that writing a strong methodology section is not
  just about listing what we did. It requires deciding what details matter, what
  should be summarized, and how to describe the workflow accurately without
  overstating the results.

  By the end of the day, the methodology section became much more organized,
  accurate, and ready to fit into the journal-paper draft. This also helped me
  see the full project pipeline more clearly, from QSAR modeling and BBB filtering
  to AChE docking and consensus-based structure validation.
