what_i_learned: |
  Today, I worked on my assigned portion of the master Alzheimer's QSAR dataset:
  the BBB Classification / B3DB dataset. My main goal was to prepare two separate
  modeling datasets: one for BBB classification and one for logBB regression.

  I started by reloading the BBB-only subset filtered from the master dataset using
  Task_Name == "BBB_Classification". The initial subset contained 7,802 rows and
  113 columns. I confirmed that the classification target,
  Experimental_BBB_Binary, had no missing labels, while the regression target,
  Experimental_logBB, was available for 1,058 compounds. This explained why the
  classification dataset would be larger than the regression dataset.

  I performed quality control on the classification labels and confirmed that they
  contained only valid values: 0 for BBB non-permeable and 1 for BBB permeable.
  The final class distribution was 2,847 non-permeable compounds and 4,955
  permeable compounds, with no rows removed.

  For the regression dataset, I created a separate subset containing only valid
  numerical Experimental_logBB values. This produced a regression dataset of
  1,058 rows, while 6,744 rows were excluded from regression only because they
  lacked logBB values. I also reviewed the logBB distribution and flagged 11
  possible outliers using the IQR method, but did not remove them.

  I used RDKit to validate all SMILES strings. All 7,802 SMILES were valid, so no
  rows were removed. I generated RDKit canonical SMILES and InChIKey values for
  structure validation and duplicate checking. I also checked for duplicate
  molecular structures and conflicting BBB labels, and no duplicates or conflicts
  were found.

  I verified that important B3DB metadata columns such as BBB_Reference,
  BBB_Group, and BBB_Comments were preserved in both the classification and
  regression datasets. I also reviewed molecular descriptor columns such as
  molecular weight, LogP, TPSA, hydrogen bond counts, ring counts, and Lipinski
  violations. All expected descriptor columns were present, with no missing or
  impossible descriptor values.

  Another important step was checking for target leakage. I confirmed that columns
  such as Predicted_BBB_Probability, Predicted_BBB_Label, and BBB_Model_Name were
  present in the dataset, but documented that they should not be used as machine
  learning features.

  By the end of the cleaning workflow, I saved two final datasets:
  Austin_BBB_classification_cleaned.csv and Austin_logBB_regression_cleaned.csv.
  The final classification dataset contained 7,802 rows and 122 columns, while
  the final regression dataset contained 1,058 rows and 124 columns. I also
  created QC reports, class distribution summaries, logBB summaries,
  duplicate/conflict reports, outlier reports, and a flagged/removed record
  summary.

  After completing the cleaning and QC workflow, I began baseline modeling for
  BBB classification using molecular descriptor features. I trained Decision
  Tree, Random Forest, Logistic Regression, and KNN models. The Random Forest
  model performed best, with an accuracy of 0.8744, precision of 0.8775, recall
  of 0.9324, and F1 score of 0.9041. This showed strong baseline performance,
  especially in identifying BBB-permeable compounds.

blockers: |
  The main challenge today was making sure the dataset was clean, traceable, and
  safe for modeling. This required checking labels, SMILES validity, duplicates,
  missing regression values, possible outliers, descriptor quality, metadata
  preservation, and target leakage before training any models.

reflection: |
  Today helped me understand the full workflow of dataset preparation, from
  filtering and target validation to SMILES validation, descriptor QC, leakage
  review, dataset saving, and initial model evaluation. A major takeaway was that
  data cleaning is not just about dropping rows or filling missing values. It is
  about carefully documenting every decision so the final modeling datasets are
  reliable, traceable, and scientifically meaningful.
