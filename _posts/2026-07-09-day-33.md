what_i_learned: |
  Today I finished reviewing two articles on KEAP1 inhibition and how it can
  activate the NRF2 pathway. This was useful because KEAP1 normally helps regulate
  NRF2, and inhibiting KEAP1 can allow NRF2-related antioxidant and protective
  responses to increase. This gave me a better understanding of why KEAP1/NRF2 is
  relevant in Alzheimer's disease research and drug discovery.

  I also worked on Ramisa's docking demo assignment, which focused on preparing
  ligands, running a protein-ligand prediction, and visualizing the result for
  interaction analysis. Since the full assignment included several videos and
  tools, I first broke it into five clear phases: ligand table creation, RDKit
  validation in Google Colab, protein-ligand prediction, ChimeraX visualization,
  and final report writing.

  I started by creating demo_ligands.csv from scratch using Python instead of
  manually building it in Google Sheets. The ligand table included Donepezil,
  Caffeine, and Resveratrol, along with their PubChem CIDs, SMILES strings,
  target/demo purposes, and reasons for selection. I then used Google Colab and
  RDKit to validate the SMILES, generate canonical SMILES, calculate molecular
  properties, and create the cleaned file demo_ligands_cleaned.csv.

  For the docking practice, I selected acetylcholinesterase (AChE) as the protein
  target and Donepezil as the ligand. This connected well with my BBB and
  drug-likeness focus because Donepezil is an Alzheimer's medication and AChE
  inhibitor. It also allowed me to discuss brain-related molecular properties
  such as molecular weight, LogP, TPSA, hydrogen bond donors and acceptors, and
  rotatable bonds.

  One issue I ran into was with AlphaFold Server. The ligand section only had a
  limited dropdown of preset ligands and did not provide a usable custom Donepezil
  SMILES option. To solve this, I switched to Protenix, which was allowed by the
  assignment. I submitted the AChE-Donepezil prediction job using the AChE protein
  sequence and Donepezil SMILES. The Protenix job completed successfully with
  strong confidence scores: pTM = 0.98 and ipTM = 0.93.

  After the prediction finished, I opened the output structure in ChimeraX. I
  visualized the AChE protein as a ribbon/cartoon structure and the Donepezil
  ligand as sticks. I learned how to interpret the atom colors in ChimeraX: red
  represented oxygen atoms, blue represented nitrogen atoms, and the beige ribbon
  represented the protein structure. The ligand appeared near a pocket-like region
  of the protein rather than floating away, which made the prediction visually
  reasonable for a docking demo.

blockers: |
  The main blocker today was working around AlphaFold Server's limited ligand
  options. Since I could not easily use a custom Donepezil SMILES input there, I
  had to switch to Protenix and adjust the workflow.

reflection: |
  Today helped me connect my earlier QSAR and drug-likeness workflow with
  downstream docking and structure prediction. I learned that successful docking
  preparation is not just about running a prediction tool. It also requires
  validating ligand inputs, checking molecular properties, choosing an appropriate
  protein target, and interpreting whether the predicted ligand placement makes
  biological sense.

  I also drafted the short report using the required assignment structure,
  including target information, ligand information, ligand preparation, tools
  used, protein-ligand result, interaction analysis, interpretation, limitations,
  and final takeaways.
