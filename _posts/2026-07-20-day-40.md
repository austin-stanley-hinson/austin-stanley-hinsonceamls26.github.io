what_i_learned: |
  Today I focused on reviewing and tightening the research paper draft for our
  Alzheimer's drug discovery project. I reread parts of the paper carefully to
  catch sections that sounded too vague, repetitive, or too AI-written,
  especially in the Literature Review and Methodology. I worked on making
  transitions smoother between topics such as multitarget Alzheimer's drug
  discovery, AlzyFinder, QSAR screening, docking, molecular dynamics, BBB
  filtering, and ADMET analysis. I also revised parts of the methodology so the
  writing focused less on internal file names or code-like details and more on
  what the methods actually mean scientifically.

  I also worked through several formatting and figure-integration issues in
  Overleaf. Since the workflow diagrams and best-model figure were still in
  .tex form before being exported, I figured out how to handle them properly in
  the paper and adjust their size so they could fit better. Later, I reviewed
  the molecular docking section and refined the wording around AChE docking with
  PDB 4EY7, donepezil-based pocket definition, multi-tool docking, and ChimeraX
  pose inspection. By the end of that part of the day, the paper felt more
  polished, more research-focused, and ready for mentor review.

  I also strengthened the structure-based validation portion of the project. I
  worked with the final 12 AChE-prioritized candidate compounds and completed the
  consensus docking analysis across four docking tools: AutoDock Vina 1.2.7,
  GNINA v1.3.3, rDock 2013.1, and DOCK6 v6.13. After confirming the correct CSV
  files for each docking tool and the candidate metadata, I built a Colab
  workflow that displayed each tool's individual compound rankings with scores
  and names before merging everything by Compound_ID. Since each docking tool
  uses a different scoring function, I avoided directly averaging raw scores and
  instead calculated a consensus rank based on within-tool rankings.

  This produced the final consensus docking table, with flutropium bromide, the
  cyclopropylmethyl hexacyclic candidate, trospium chloride, eperisone
  hydrochloride, and pipenzolate bromide emerging as the top five compounds for
  deeper pose-level inspection.

  I then moved beyond docking scores by generating binding-pocket visuals and
  interaction-analysis outputs for the top five consensus candidates. I prepared
  the receptor and top-ranked Vina poses, opened them in UCSF ChimeraX, and
  created binding-pocket visualizations showing the docked ligands inside the
  AChE pocket. I used ChimeraX to inspect nearby residues within 4 Å, identify
  hydrogen-bond candidates, and record close-contact residues for each ligand.

  The interaction analysis showed that flutropium bromide had a hydrogen-bond
  candidate involving HIS381, while the cyclopropylmethyl hexacyclic candidate
  had a hydrogen-bond candidate involving ALA528. The other top candidates did
  not show detected hydrogen bonds under the applied ChimeraX criteria, but they
  still had multiple close contacts with pocket residues such as HIS381, THR383,
  LEU380, PHE531, PHE535, ALA528, ARG393, and ASP400.

blockers: |

  details.
